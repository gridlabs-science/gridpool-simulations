## Protocol Context To Preserve

Current GridPool consensus in the runtime repo is V2.1 snapshot/reserve consensus:

- Every Bitcoin block creates a payout snapshot from the unpaid Work Set.
- A GridPool-found block pays the active snapshot.
- Paid proof IDs are removed once paid; unpaid reserve proofs remain eligible.
- Slot 0 belongs to the block finder and receives the remainder plus transaction fees.
- There are 300 total payout slots.
- With the support fee enabled, one post-slot-0 slot is the Grid Labs support output and up to 298 shared proof slots are used.
- With support fee disabled, there are up to 299 shared proof slots.
- Default reserve depth is `3x`, so default unpaid Work Set limit is `897` proofs.

Important recent V2.1 theoretical decision:

- Do **not** allow retroactive stale-branch replacement across a local Bitcoin-block snapshot boundary.
- Previous-parent proofs that arrive after the local node observed the new Bitcoin block should be rejected or quarantined from the canonical reserve.
- Valid current-parent proofs mined against divergent retained payout snapshots can still merge forward into the unpaid Work Set after full validation.
- Established nodes should not replace an already-active snapshot merely because a peer later presents a "heavier" branch for the same boundary.

Why this matters:

- It closes the practical entry door for intentional delayed-snapshot stale-branch attacks.
- It preserves ordinary split recovery by merging valid current-parent work forward.
- It reduces reliance on a global "heaviest list wins" branch replacement rule.

## Current Findings To Preserve

### 1. 300-slot payout lists materially reduce actual BTC payout variance

The important metric is actual BTC paid, not just "slot inclusion."

If the team hashrate doubles, a fixed-size miner may receive half as many slots per team block, but team blocks arrive twice as often. Expected BTC stays the same. Variance changes because payouts become more or less clumped.

Best source:

```text
reports/generated/sweeps/payout_variance_fee_long/
```

Useful example already drafted in `docs/july-17-developer-meeting-findings.md`:

- `1 PH` miner, 300 slots, team multiplier `10000`: variance reduction versus solo about `271.7x`; zero-payout probability about `11.9%`.
- Same miner, 100 slots: variance reduction about `96.7x`; zero-payout probability about `48.8%`.
- Same miner, 10 slots: variance reduction about `10.0x`; zero-payout probability about `93.1%`.

Meeting claim:

> A 300-slot payout list substantially reduces payout variance compared with smaller lists once the team finds blocks often enough for the miner to receive recurring shared payouts.

Caveat:

> Tiny miners on tiny teams remain lottery-like. GridPool improves variance as team block cadence and payout-list width become relevant.

### 2. Pool hopping cannot create fake work, but zero-fee outside options can create a small free-option effect

Best sources:

```text
reports/generated/sweeps/pool_hopping_external_mode_299_long/
reports/generated/sweeps/pool_hopping_solo_target_share_299_long/
```

Current interpretation:

- Pool hopping cannot forge shares or steal slots.
- A miner can only retain claims to proof-of-work it already contributed.
- With a zero-fee deterministic FPPS-like outside option, simulations show a small free-option effect because already-earned GridPool proofs remain payable while the miner earns elsewhere.
- Realistic outside fees reduce or erase the effect in tested runs.
- Zero-fee solo outside mining is noisy because variance changes substantially; use careful wording.

Safe claim:

> Simulations found at most a small free-option effect under idealized zero-fee outside mining. This is not a way to forge work or steal other miners' shares, and realistic fees, variance, and operational costs can erase or reverse it.

Avoid:

> Pool hopping is impossible.

## Delayed Snapshot Attack Model

Script:

```bash
python3 run_delayed_snapshot_attack.py --help
```

Purpose:

Estimate economics of an attacker deliberately mining stale templates after a Bitcoin block in order to reveal a delayed attacker-favorable branch.

Important correction already incorporated:

- Low-difficulty early slots on a weak branch should **not** be valued as guaranteed payout.
- They are discounted by probability they survive in the top shared slots until the next GridPool block.
- This uses the share-survival insight supplied by the user:

```text
P(survives top 300) = 1 - (D_network / (D_s + D_network))^300
```

Current survival-discounted examples:

- Pool share `0.0001`, 51% attacker, 1 stale block: reward/cost about `4.25x`.
- Pool share `0.001`, 51% attacker, 1 stale block: reward/cost about `2.14x`.
- Pool share `0.01`, 51% attacker, 1 stale block: reward/cost about `0.94x`.
- Pool share `0.03`, 51% attacker, 1 stale block: reward/cost about `0.50x`.
- Pool share `0.10`, 51% attacker, 1 stale block: reward/cost about `0.18x`.

Interpretation:

- If retroactive stale-branch replacement is allowed, the attack can look profitable at very small GridPool scale.
- The edge shrinks or becomes negative as GridPool becomes a meaningful share of network hashrate.
- The intended V2.1 rule blocks the attack path by rejecting/quarantining late previous-parent proofs rather than trying to out-score them.

Meeting posture:

> The stale-branch model is useful because it shows why the V2.1 snapshot-boundary rule matters. We are not relying on the attack being expensive at tiny launch scale; we are closing the entry condition by making late stale-parent proofs ineligible for retroactive snapshot replacement.

## Important Concepts For Another Agent

### Share difficulty distribution

For proof difficulties above a floor:

```text
P(D >= x | D >= floor) = floor / x
```

Equivalent uniform order-stat framing:

```text
V = d_min / D ~ Uniform(0, 1)
```

The `m`-th best share is an order statistic. The difficulty of the lowest retained share in a full reserve is a strong estimator of cumulative work/hashrate over a comparable observation window.

This is why `bottom_1_times_count` performed well in honest consensus-selection audits.

### Actual payout variance is the right utility metric

Do not talk only about "probability of getting a slot." A miner cares about actual BTC paid over time.

Team size affects:

- team block cadence;
- miner's fraction of team hashrate;
- number of slots per team block;
- clumping/variance of payouts.

Expected value cancels cleanly under fair rules. Variance is the key.

### V2.1 is not "heaviest list always wins"

The current desired language:

> Within a snapshot boundary, valid proofs can be merged and sorted. Across a locally observed Bitcoin-block boundary, late previous-parent proofs do not retroactively rewrite the active payout snapshot.

That distinction matters. It is the answer to the stale-branch attack concern.
